  • Question 1

    What is antimicrobial resistance?

    Answer

    Antimicrobial resistance is the ability of bacteria to resist antibiotics. Bacteria develop the ability to survive the exposure to antibiotics designed to kill them, making the treatment last longer or even impossible, in some cases.

    Antimicrobial resistance occurs due to changes or mutations in the DNA of the bacteria, which acquires antibiotic resistance genes from other bacteria. This transformation enables the bacteria to adapt to the effects of antibiotics, surviving the treatment. Bacteria adapt to antibiotics, not people themselves. So, when an antibiotic is used, bacteria which have developed the resistance remain unaffected and survive the cure. Resistant bacteria also continue to grow, spreading rapidly and making other bacteria resistant too.

  • Answer

    Switzerland has an intermediate position regarding antimicrobial resistance, compared to other countries.
    Considering the population, the number of infections due to resistant bacteria is lower than in France, Italy or Great Britain, but higher than in the Netherlands or Scandinavian countries.
    The antibiotic prescriptions in the inpatient and outpatient sector has remained stable in all regions in the past years. The higher rate of outpatient antibiotic consumption in French and Italian-speaking regions may be caused by prescription cultures in the different linguistic regions.

    Answer

    Resistance to antibiotics cause prolonged illness and longer hospital stays, increasing the cost of treatments for both, the patient and the health care system.
    Due to the cure, affected patients also need to face changes in their life habits, and in the worse case scenario, antimicrobial resistance can lead to disability and death.

    Conscious and correct use of antibiotics is the first and main help in tackling the problem of resistance.
    Antibiotics need to be prescribed by doctors and prescription indications need to be carefully followed. It is important to adhere to the dosage and duration of the treatment prescribed, as it guarantees optimal effectiveness.
    The cure doesn’t have to be stopped prematurely, even if the patient starts feeling better. No doses must be missed from intaking. Prescribed treatments are also personal, and do not have to be shared with others or reused in different conditions. It is a good habit to bring leftovers back to pharmacies and not to dispose of them in the household waste, as they can end up affecting the environment.

    In the long term, alternative solutions to prevent and treat bacterial infections need to be found. However, in the short term, we still need antibiotics and it is important to learn from past mistakes to preserve any new antibiotics to maintain and enhance their power. Responsive and prudent use of antibiotics can slow down the process. Discovering new antibiotics can help but it is not the solution to the antimicrobial resistance problem, as resistance will eventually develop to new antibiotics as well.
