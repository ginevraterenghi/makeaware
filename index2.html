  • Question 1

    What is antimicrobial resistance?

    Answer

    Antimicrobial resistance is the ability of microorganisms to resist antimicrobials. Microorganisms develop the ability to survive the exposure to antimicrobials designed to kill them, making the treatment last longer or even impossible, in some cases. Microorganisms, or microbes, are considered all the organisms of microscopic dimension. Antimicrobials, on the opposite, are all the substances, naturally or synthetically origingated, that kills microorganisms or ihibit their growth. There are several categories of antimicrobials, based on microorganism genres they are supposed to inhibit: antibiotics for bacteria, antivirals for virus, antigungals for fungi and antimalarials, among others, for parasites.
    Antibiotic resistance is a specific form of antimicobial resistance which occurs when bacteria persist to the antibiotics use. Antibiotics are considered the major cause of antimicrobial resistance, and this is mainly due to their overused or misused.

    Instead of disappearing, resistant bacteria keep growing and reproduce themselves. The fact that available antibiotics are becoming less effective make diseases relying on antibiotics increasing.

    At the end, microbes are not treated and people affected need a different treatment, not always available. Resistance is a characteristic of microorganisms and not humans, but the trasmission of resistant bacteria can occur among humans, and animals too.

  • Question 2

    How many resistant bacteria have been detected Worldwide?

    Answer

    Worldwide, in 2019, 4.95 million deaths were estimated to be associated with resistant bacteria, of which 1.27 million were directly attributable to bacteria resistance to antimicrobials (Murray et al. 2022).
    India is the coutry that registered the highest number of resistant bacteria identified. Followed by the United States. Brazil, Spain, Italy, Mexico and China also report a significant number.
    The number of tests runned to identify the resistant bacteria, combined to the number of resistant bacteria identified, determine the persentage of resistant bacteria found out of the number of samples taken.
    Out of the high number of resistant bacteria identified, India took quite a lot of samples, which means that the probability of finding resistant bacteria was higher than countries that didn't run so many tests. Out of the samples taken, 34% reveald the presence of microbes resistant to antibiotics. The darker the colour of the bubble, the highest the number of samples taken.
    The United States runned the highest number of tests, showing a positive rate lower than the India's one (9%). Central European countries has an average of positivity between 10 and 20%. North Europe didn't run many tests, and this can be the reason why there are not so many cases of resistant bacteria registered (3 to 7%).

    It is important to specify that figures refer to microbes and not people. One person test can reveal more than one resistant microbe. Availability to antibiotics and number of habitants should also be relevant criteria to compare the number of resistant bacteria with.

  • Question 3

    Which geographic region does register the highest number of resistant bacteria in Switzerland?

    Answer

    Compared to other countries and considering the number of inhabitants and samples taken, Switzerland has an intermediate position regarding the number of antibiotic resistant microbes detected. In 2019, 158 resistant bacteria have been found out of 1'958 tests gathered, with a positivity of 8%. In Europe, among the available data, only the Nordic coutries show a lower rate. Border nations, as Germany, France and Italy register higher numbers.
    Switzerland combine different socio-cultural regions, the influence of which influence the antibiotic prescription practices, especially in outpatient settings. French- and Italian-speaking cantons consume of antibiotics is higher than German-speaking areas.

  • Question 4

    How will antimicrobial resistance affect our lives?

    Answer

    Resistance to antibiotics has serious consequences, causing prolonged illness and hospital stays, increasing the cost of treatments for both, patients and the health care system.
    Tratments bring habits changes, affecting people and families' lives. And, in the worst scenario, antimicrobial resistance leads to disability or death.
    In the long term, alternative solutions to prevent and treat bacterial infections need to be further studied and found. However, as antibiotics can not be complitely replaced in the short term, learning how to preserve medicine effects is fundamental, as a correct and concious use of antibiotics can slow down the resistance dynamic.
    It has been estimated that 10 millions of people will die due to antimicrobial resistance, by 2050. Antimicrobial resistance risks to become the first cause of death, overtaking the ones caused by cancer.
    However, there are no esaustive estimates as a broad range of factors contribute to define this information. But, it makes clear that the spread of antimicrobial resistance, including antibiotic resistance, is an urgent problem requiring a global response.
